## LinkedIn

Foreign disinformation isn't a national security threat. Or rather, it isn't only one.

From inside an Indigenous community, it's a sovereignty issue. The Senate framed it the first way in its May 2026 report. CSIS agrees. Both are right. Neither describes what the pattern actually looks like at the ground level.

OIATC just published its position on what an Indigenous-led counter-disinformation response actually looks like. Three things change the shape of the threat inside a community:

1. Existing distrust of federal institutions is fertile ground. Foreign operators don't invent narratives from scratch. They amplify legitimate Indigenous critique and steer it toward conclusions that serve a foreign agenda. The mechanism is hijacking, not invention.

2. Information channels are concentrated and intergenerational. A single false post on a band Facebook page, local radio, or family network travels fast and acquires authority through repetition.

3. Polarization narratives target minority communities directly. Indigenous people are inside the target set the Senate report describes.

The response built on a sovereignty frame looks different from one built on national security:

Sovereignty and culture come first. Anishinaabemowin pre-bunks are not decoration.

Trusted messengers carry the message. Elders, youth digital monitors, recognized community communicators. Imported voices don't land.

Pre-bunk before rebut. Inoculate against the narrative pattern before it spreads. Repeating a false claim to debunk it often amplifies it.

OIATC is convening this work at the umbrella so a single community doesn't have to stand up a five-workstream response alone. If you're a First Nation, an Indigenous organization, a federal program officer, a fact-checker, or a university research group on the information environment, the conversation is open.
