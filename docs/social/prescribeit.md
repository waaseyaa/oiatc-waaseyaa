## Facebook: Fullstackdev42 page (Buffer)

Ottawa spent nearly $300-million on PrescribeIT, the national "axe the fax" e-prescribing program, then shut it off after fewer than five per cent of prescriptions ever went through it. The easy lesson is that the fax won, or that one vendor charged too much. Neither is the real failure.

The real failure is governance. The system was chosen at the top, built by a vendor that kept the rights to it, and pushed down toward the people meant to use it, including First Nations communities who had no say in choosing it and none in ending it.

I wrote OIATC's position on what that teaches. Public money should buy public control, and a standard adopted by vendors is still not the same thing as consent.

## LinkedIn

Ottawa just shut down PrescribeIT, the federal e-prescribing program, after spending nearly $300-million for use that never passed five per cent of prescriptions. The headline invites the wrong lesson: that the fax won, that digital health is too hard, or that one vendor overcharged. None of those is the real failure.

The failure is one of governance. The system was selected federally, built by a vendor that retained about 85 per cent of the intellectual property, and switched off from the centre. The public paid for it and holds none of the rights. First Nations communities could lead a local rollout and still hold no authority over the architecture, the data, or the off-switch.

OIATC's new position draws three lessons: a system imposed is a system unused, public money should buy public control, and a standard is not a substitute for consent. The question we keep asking does not change: who chose this, and who can change it.
